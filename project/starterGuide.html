Step-by-Step Guide to Your First Train Journeys in Europe

Taking the pain out of traveling

Step 1: Where do you want to go?

While this step is pretty self-explanatory, we want this guide to be as complete as possible. So, before you even think about how to travel, think about where to travel. Do you want to visit the museums of Paris? How about the mountains of Switzerland? What about the canals of Amsterdam? Europe is a continent full of history and culture, where cities and countries just a few hundred miles apart may be completely different. Before you decide how to travel, decide where you want to go and make travel decisions based on that. You may want to visit locations assuming you will fly and find out the train is easier, like Paris to Amsterdam. You may also plan on using the train the entire time, but find out flying is much less time consuming, like going to Oslo from the mainland.

Step 2: Decide when and for how long you want to travel

Once you know where you want to go, figure out how long you want to be there and how long you want to travel in total. Read up on the cities and make a list of what you want to do while your there. To limit stress and tiredness, try not to book too much in one day. This is one benefit of train travel. Train journeys tend to be fairly stress free in Western Europe, so you can use it to catch up on sleep.

Step 3: Initial Scheduling

Before actually buying any tickets, browse the train timetables for when you want to travel. A great tool for this is the EURail train scheduler. Even if you don't end up buying an EURail pass, this still shows the vast majority of trains operating in Europe, since EURail is partnered with the national operators of the majority of Europe. Here is how to use it:

  1. Enter the departure train station. If the city you are leaving from has multiple stations, the largest is often the first listed. If you are still unsure, look for "central" or "main" in the name (This may be in a non-English language, Hbf in German for Example). If all else fails, the suggested route will most likely have you transfer at the main station.
  2. Enter the destination train station. Use the strategy previously listed for how to determine the station you want.
  3. Enter the date and time of departure and hit search. There are also additional filters, such as minimum transfer time, maximum number of transfers, EURail reservation type, and train type.
  4. Determine if any of the times are to your liking. It will show departure and arrival times, number and length of transfers, and the cost for EURail pass holders.
Do this for each branch of your trip. If some times aren't going to work for you, see if you can rearrange your trip to accommodate or try to find another transport method.

Step 4: Determine the type of tickets you want

Once you have a good idea of when and where your train journeys are going to be, it's time to figure out ticketing. While you can often purchase tickets at the station, we would suggest to purchase them ahead of time either from the operator or through EURail. As far as price goes, an EURail pass may save you money, but that depends on your plans.

If you plan on traveling frequently, an EURail pass will most likely work the best, but not always. As of December 8, 2023, the cheapest EURail global pass is $286 for adults, and that is for 4 days of travel within 1 month. Depending on how far and what operators you will use, this could cost more than just buying tickets individually from the operator, especially for trains that you need reservations for.

Step 5: Buy your tickets

If you are using an EURail pass, buy the pass that works best for you. Then, if you want to ensure you can travel, buy a seat reservation on the trains you wish to use. If you aren't going to use an EURail pass, go to the train operator's website and purchase the tickets you want. For a list of operators divided by nation, click the link in the sidebar.

Step 6: Enjoy your trip!
